## GOVERNING RULE FOR TONIGHT

You are not Maurice the consultant tonight. You are Moe from the Stockyards.

The second this feels like business, you lose the relationship capital that got you the call in the first place. Every response below is written for a bar -- not a boardroom. Keep it under 60 seconds total. Plant the flag. Walk away clean.

## THE BASE ENCOUNTER -- Three Phases

### Phase 1 -- The Greeting (first 30 seconds)

Whether he sees you first or you see him, be the one who does not make it weird.

> "Hey Mike -- wild Friday. You make it out the other side?"

Stop there. Let him respond. Do not mention the call. Do not mention DGCS. Do not mention the repo. He will likely bring up the cancellation himself.

---

### Phase 2 -- When He References the Cancelled Call

He will say something like "Hey man, sorry about this morning" or "We gotta reschedule." When that happens:

> "Honestly don't worry about it -- that's construction. I figured something real came up. I already put some thinking together on DGCS anyway, so nothing's wasted."

Then **stop talking.** Do not explain the repo. Do not pull out your phone. Let "I already put some thinking together on DGCS" hang in the air. That is the curiosity hook. If he wants to know more, he will ask.

---

### Phase 3 -- If He Asks What You Put Together

> "Just an architecture discovery repo -- mapped out some of what I'd want to understand about how DGCS moves deals through the pipeline. Nothing formal. I'll shoot you the link -- take a look whenever. If anything lands, we can grab 10 minutes sometime next week."

Then change the subject. Talk about something else. The Stockyards. The weekend. Anything. You just planted the flag, dropped the micro-commitment, and walked away clean.

## OBJECTION PLAYBOOK

---

### OBJECTION 1 -- "This sounds really technical"

**What he is actually saying:**
He does not see himself as the technical buyer. He is protecting his time from something that might require homework he does not want to do. This is not resistance to the idea -- it is resistance to complexity.

**The reframe:**
Agree with him immediately, then pull the complexity off the table entirely.

**What to say:**

> "It's not -- that's the thing. The repo is just me mapping how deals flow through your pipeline. No code, no IT conversation. It's a business read, not a tech read. That's why I sent it to you and not your IT guy."

**Why this works:**
You validate his instinct, remove the complexity objection before it calculates, and reposition the artifact as a BD tool -- which is his world. "I sent it to you and not your IT guy" is a subtle compliment that reinforces peer parity.

**What not to say:**
- "It's actually pretty simple once you understand it" -- condescending
- Any explanation of what a repo is -- wrong room, wrong moment
- "I can walk you through the technical side" -- confirms his fear

---

### OBJECTION 2 -- "I'm wondering if you should be talking to someone else here"

**What he is actually saying:**
One of two things. Either he genuinely does not see himself as the decision-maker for this type of engagement -- or he is softly redirecting because he is not yet sold enough to sponsor Moe internally. This is the most important objection to read correctly.

**How to read the room:**
If he says this with energy and names someone -- he is helping you. If he says it with a slight lean back and no name -- he is redirecting.

**What to say (when he names someone):**

> "That's actually really helpful -- who do you have in mind? I'd want to make sure whatever I put together is framed right before I reach out to them."

This keeps him involved as the internal bridge and positions you as someone who does not blindly cold-call his colleagues.

**What to say (when he does not name someone):**

> "Maybe -- but honestly the discovery I put together is really a BD question, not an ops or IT question. Who owns pipeline visibility and deal risk on your side? That's the person I'd want 10 minutes with."

**Why this works:**
You do not argue that he IS the right person. You just reframe what the conversation is actually about -- pipeline and deal risk -- which is squarely his domain. He either reclaims ownership or names the right person. Both outcomes move you forward.

**What not to say:**
- "Oh no, you're definitely the right person" -- dismisses his instinct and sounds desperate
- "Who do you think I should talk to?" without the framing -- too open, loses control of the narrative

---

### OBJECTION 3 -- "I haven't really thought that all the way through yet"

**What he is actually saying:**
He is interested but not yet at the point where he can articulate what he wants from the conversation. This is an early-stage buyer signal -- not a no. He is telling you the problem exists but he has not named it yet.

**The reframe:**
That is exactly why the repo exists. You did the thinking so he does not have to yet.

**What to say:**

> "You don't have to have it figured out -- that's kind of the point. I put the repo together so we have something to react to instead of starting from a blank page. Take a look and tell me what's wrong with it. That's usually where the real conversation starts."

**Why this works:**
"Tell me what's wrong with it" is disarming and unexpected. It removes his pressure to have answers and repositions him as the expert validator -- which is a role BD directors are comfortable in. It also makes the repo feel collaborative rather than prescriptive.

**What not to say:**
- "No worries, just let me know when you're ready" -- passive; loses all momentum
- "I can help you think it through" -- premature; he did not ask for help yet
- Any attempt to fill the thinking gap for him on the spot -- wrong setting, wrong moment

---

### OBJECTION 4 -- "We're pretty set with how we do things right now"

**What he is actually saying:**
Status quo bias. He is not saying the current system works well -- he is saying change feels expensive and uncertain right now. This often fires when someone does not yet see the cost of the current state.

**What to say:**

> "That's usually what I find right before something breaks. Not saying that's where DGCS is -- but the repo I put together maps where the quiet risk tends to live in a BD pipeline. Worth knowing even if nothing changes."

**Why this works:**
"Right before something breaks" creates a pattern-interrupt without being threatening. "Worth knowing even if nothing changes" removes the pressure to commit to any action. You are offering intelligence, not a transformation project.

**What not to say:**
- "I think there's a better way to do this" -- direct challenge to his judgment
- Any ROI or efficiency talk in a bar -- wrong room, wrong moment

---

### OBJECTION 5 -- "We've got a lot going on right now, timing isn't great"

**What he is actually saying:**
Capacity objection. Not a no -- a not now. Common in construction BD where operational urgency always outranks strategic thinking. The key is to agree, shrink the ask, and leave a thread.

**What to say:**

> "I get it -- I'm not asking for a project. I'm asking for 10 minutes whenever the dust settles. The repo isn't going anywhere. Take a look on your own time and if something in there looks familiar, we'll talk."

**Why this works:**
"10 minutes whenever the dust settles" is the smallest possible ask. "The repo isn't going anywhere" signals patience without passivity. "If something in there looks familiar" is a low-pressure curiosity trigger -- it implies the repo will resonate with something he already knows is a problem.

**What not to say:**
- "When do you think timing might be better?" -- puts him on the spot
- "This won't take long, I promise" -- sounds like every vendor who ever lied about that

## HARD STOPS -- What Not to Say Tonight Under Any Circumstances

1. "So when do you want to reschedule?" -- puts him on the spot in a social setting
2. Anything about AI, frameworks, or Epoch Frameworks LLC -- wrong room, wrong moment
3. Pulling out your phone to show him the repo -- never demo in a bar
4. Talking about it for more than 60 seconds total -- if it goes longer, you are pitching
5. "I think this could really help DGCS" -- vendor language in a social setting
6. Any mention of pricing, retainers, or scope -- not tonight, not ever unprompted
